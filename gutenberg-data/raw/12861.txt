Summary = """
James Hutton's 'Theory of the Earth' is a foundational work in the field of geology, originally published in 1795. Hutton, often called the "father of modern geology," presents his revolutionary theory of uniformitarianism - the idea that the Earth's geological features were formed by continuous processes operating over immense periods of time, rather than by catastrophic events. This work examines the composition, dissolution, and restoration of land, the consolidation of strata, and the cyclical nature of geological processes. The book includes Hutton's responses to critics, including Mr. Kirwan's objections to the igneous (volcanic) origin of certain rocks. This work fundamentally challenged prevailing catastrophist theories and biblical chronologies of the Earth's age, proposing instead that the Earth is far older than previously believed, with "no vestige of a beginning, no prospect of an end."
