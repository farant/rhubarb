# Paper: [Ozsváth & Szabó 2004] "Holomorphic disks and genus bounds"
**File:** 0311496v3.pdf
**Published:** Geometry & Topology, Volume 8 (2004), pp. 311–334

## Paths Not Taken

- **Tight contact structures (p.314, Section 1.1):** Authors prove non-vanishing of the contact invariant c(xi) for weakly symplectically semi-fillable contact structures (Theorem 4.2). They then explicitly flag: "It is very interesting to see if this non-vanishing result can be generalized to the case of tight contact structures." They note this requires twisted coefficients when b_1(Y) > 0. This was a major open direction at the time — the tight-vs-fillable gap is where interesting topology hides.

- **Elementary proof of Morse-theoretic genus (p.316, after Corollary 1.5):** "It would also be interesting to find a more elementary proof of the above result" — referring to Corollary 1.5 which characterizes Seifert genus purely in terms of simultaneous trajectories on Heegaard diagrams (no gauge theory, no symplectic geometry). They're saying: this result SHOULD have a combinatorial proof but we don't have one.

- **Direct twisted-Floer approach to genus detection (p.330, Section 5):** "One approach to Theorem 1.2 would directly relate knot Floer homology with the twisted Floer homology of the zero-surgery." They explicitly choose NOT to take this route, opting for an untwisted approach adapted from the proof of Theorem 1.1 instead. The twisted approach would be more natural but apparently harder at the time.

- **Z/pZ-L-spaces and taut foliations (p.329):** They introduce the notion of a Z/pZ-L-space (HF has rank |H_1| over Z/pZ) and show these cannot support taut foliations. This is almost thrown away in a paragraph — but it's a finiteness condition on homology that obstructs geometric structure.

## Technical Machinery

- **The proof chain for Theorem 1.1 is the real content.** It chains together four independent deep results: Gabai (taut foliations from Thurston norm) → Eliashberg-Thurston (foliations give semi-fillable contact structures) → Eliashberg (semi-fillable embeds in closed symplectic 4-manifold) → Ozsváth-Szabó (non-vanishing of Phi on symplectic 4-manifolds implies non-vanishing of HF). Each link is a different field. The "proof" is really an assembly of a pipeline across symplectic geometry, foliation theory, contact topology, and pseudo-holomorphic curve theory.

- **Cobordism functoriality as the engine (Section 3, pp.320–325):** The key technical tool is that HF is functorial under 4-dimensional cobordisms. A cobordism W: Y_1 → Y_2 induces maps on all variants (HF^hat, HF^+, HF^-, HF^infinity) compatible with exact sequences. The composition law (Equation 2) lets you factor maps through intermediate 3-manifolds. This functoriality is what makes HF computable in principle — but the composition law is more subtle than it looks because Spin^c structures over the composite need not split uniquely.

- **Mixed invariant Phi_{X,s} (pp.321–322):** For closed 4-manifolds with b_2^+ > 1, they construct an invariant by cutting X along a 3-manifold N and composing maps HF^-(S^3) → HF^+(N) → HF^+(S^3). This "mixed" map factors through HF^+_red(N). The invariant is analogous to Seiberg-Witten invariant and satisfies the same non-vanishing theorem for symplectic manifolds (Theorem 1.1 of [34]).

- **Giroux two-handle construction (Section 4, pp.325–326):** The contact invariant c(xi) is built using Giroux's correspondence between contact structures and open book decompositions. Stabilize the open book to get connected binding with genus g > 1, perform surgery on binding → get cobordism W_0: Y → Y_0 where Y_0 fibers over S^1. Then c(xi) = image of canonical generator under the cobordism map. This is independent of open book choice.

- **Twisted coefficients machinery (Section 3.1, p.323):** For b_1(Y) > 0, they use Floer homology with coefficients in modules over Z[H^1(Y;Z)]. The differential picks up weights T^{integral of omega} where omega is a 2-cocycle. This "twisting" is what makes the theory detect non-trivial Thurston norm even when untwisted HF is trivial (illustrated by zero-surgery on trefoil, p.329).

- **Künneth principle for knot Floer homology (p.331):** For genus 1 knots, the proof uses HFK(K#K, 2) ≅ HFK(K, 1) tensor HFK(K, 1). Since K#K has genus 2 and HFK is non-trivial there, HFK(K, 1) must be non-trivial. This tensor-product splitting is a powerful computational tool.

## Speculative Remarks

- **HF ≅ SW monopole Floer conjecture (p.316, Section 1.3):** "Although the construction of Heegaard Floer homology is completely different from the construction of Seiberg-Witten monopole Floer homology, the invariants are conjectured to be isomorphic." They note this should be viewed in light of Taubes's theorem relating SW and Gromov-Witten invariants. This was later proved (Kutluhan-Lee-Taubes and Colin-Ghiggini-Honda, ~2010–2012).

- **Némethi's characterization of L-spaces (p.314):** Mentioned almost in passing: "a three-manifold Y is an L-space which is obtained as a plumbing of spheres if and only if it is the link of a rational surface singularity." This connects homological simplicity (L-space) to algebraic geometry (singularity theory) — a bridge that's still not fully exploited.

## DKC Overlaps

- **Kauffman states = simultaneous trajectories (p.316):** This is stated as a parenthetical remark but it's a genuine bridge to DKC territory. The paper notes that for a knot projection with a distinguished edge, the compatible Heegaard diagram's simultaneous trajectories "can be identified with the 'Kauffman states' for the knot projection; see [15] for an account of Kauffman states." This means the Morse-theoretic genus formula (Corollary 1.5: genus = min over diagrams of max s(x) over simultaneous trajectories) is secretly a statement about Kauffman states. The function s: X → Z on states, with its equidistribution property mod 2, is measuring something that parallels what DKC measures via bracket evaluation on state sums.

- **Vanishing outside a depth range / adjunction inequality (pp.330–331):** HFK(K, i) = 0 for all |i| > g(K). The "filtration level" i is determined by which Kauffman state you're in. This is structurally parallel to DKC's observation that crossing depth drives computational power — there's a range of "depth" (filtration level) outside which the invariant vanishes, and the boundary of this range IS the genus. In DKC terms: the computational capacity of a knot is bounded by its genus, and the genus is exactly where the interesting states live.

- **L-spaces as "computationally trivial" objects (Theorem 1.4, p.315):** L-spaces have HF of minimal rank (free Z-module of rank |H_1|) — they're the 3-manifolds where Heegaard Floer carries the least information. Theorem 1.4 says these have no taut foliations and no symplectic semi-fillings with disconnected boundary. This parallels DKC's finite/infinite group dichotomy: at lattice roots of unity (finite group, like the binary octahedral at zeta_8), there's a hard capacity ceiling — the analog of being an "L-space" in the computational sense. Non-lattice roots (infinite groups) have logarithmic scaling — they're the analog of manifolds with rich HF.

- **Zero-surgery captures all genus information (Theorem 1.2 proof, p.330):** The 3-manifold S^3_0(K) obtained by zero-framed surgery on K determines the Seifert genus completely via HFK. Zero-surgery is intimately related to the writhe: different framings correspond to different amounts of writhe correction. The fact that ZERO surgery (= no writhe correction, in a sense) is the one that captures genus resonates with DKC's emphasis on raw bracket (not Jones polynomial, which normalizes away writhe).

## Surprises

- **The Kauffman states remark is almost hidden.** Given that this is one of the most important papers in modern knot theory, it's striking that the connection to Kauffman states (which is where DKC lives) is mentioned in a single sentence on p.316 with a "see [15]" citation. It's not developed at all. The authors clearly know this connection exists but treat it as a curiosity rather than a feature.

- **The paper is entirely gauge-theory-free despite proving a gauge-theory result.** Section 1.3 explicitly notes this. The original proof (Kronheimer-Mrowka) used Seiberg-Witten equations. Ozsváth-Szabó get the same results using holomorphic disks in symmetric products — a completely different analytical setup. Yet they conjecture the two theories are isomorphic. This suggests the results are "topological truths" that multiple analytical frameworks can access, which is philosophically aligned with DKC's finding that bracket evaluations at specific roots capture computational truths that are independent of the particular algebraic framework.

- **Eliashberg's embedding theorem as the lynchpin.** The entire paper depends on one result from Eliashberg [3] that appeared essentially simultaneously (Geom. Topol. 8, 2004, pp. 277–293 — published in the SAME issue of the SAME journal). Without it, the proof chain breaks. The acknowledgements explicitly thank Eliashberg for "explaining his result to us." This is a case where a single new tool unlocked an entire line of results.

- **The trefoil zero-surgery as a cautionary example (p.329).** The reduced HF with untwisted coefficients for zero-surgery on the trefoil is TRIVIAL, but the manifold admits a taut foliation. You NEED twisted coefficients to detect this. This means the "obvious" version of the theory misses geometric structure that the twisted version catches — a concrete example where the naive approach fails and you need to work harder.
