## 1. CASEY'S FAITH BEAT: The Thread Rests

### Why It Should Rest

1x05 was the biggest faith moment in the series so far — and by a wide margin. Two extended beats: the factual ("A man who died for everyone") and the personal ("The God who remembers everything"). Pliny *opened a file* on Casey's faith and signaled he'll be returning to it for evenings to come.

The season outline places the next explicit beat at 1x07-08: Casey says grace, O'Neill notices. That's the right instinct. After 1x05's escalation, the thread needs to breathe. The audience needs time to process what Pliny's "I have more questions" means, and to anticipate the next conversation rather than receive it immediately.

### The Residual Visual

Casey's faith should be *present but unremarked*. A single shot:

During the interning discussion — when the team debates canonical copies, when Methos draws the distinction between interning and assimilation — Casey is at his workbench. The crucifix is visible behind him. No dialogue. No one looks at it. But the composition places it in the same frame as the word "canonical."

**Why this works:** The audience who caught 1x05's two beats will hear "canonical copy" and see the crucifix in the same shot. They'll make the connection themselves: the Canon. Canonical scripture. The Word made singular. The resonance is entirely in the viewer's mind. Casey doesn't say it. Nobody says it. The camera says it.

This is the Nightcrawler approach at its most disciplined: the thread is present, the signal is zero, and the audience does all the work.

### The Escalation Pattern (Updated)

| Episode | Faith Beat | Mode | Who Notices |
|---------|-----------|------|-------------|
| 1x01 | Casey crosses himself before the mission | Visual only | Nobody (the audience) |
| 1x02 | Crucifix on workbench, framed with Data's green eyes | Visual + compositional | The camera |

| 1x05 | Pliny's two beats: "A man who died for everyone" / "The God who remembers everything" | Extended verbal — about the *faith* | Pliny (the scholar) |

The pattern after the 1x05 escalation: retreat to visual, then slowly re-engage through a different observer (O'Neill) in a different register (practice, not theology). The thread spirals outward rather than climbing in a straight line.

## 2. KIRK STINGER: Standardization

### Progression from 1x05

| Episode | Stinger | Growth Stage | Computing Parallel |
|---------|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1x01 | Kirk and Data arrive | Seed | First allocation |
| 1x02 | Stolen laptop. First line of code. | Germination | First write |
| 1x03 | Server farm. Guard doesn't remember. | First root | First persistent storage |
| 1x04 | Strip mall office. 4/31. Workers with green screens. | First branches | First collection (Xar) |
| 1x05 | Directory board. Names by function. "TABULARIUM." 5/31. | First organization | First index (hash table) |
| **1x06** | **Protocol document. Standardized terminology. "LEXICON." 6/31.** | **First standardization** | **First interning (canonical copies)** |

### The Scene

**Location:** Earth. Night. A conference room in a real office building — not a strip mall, not a co-working space. Kirk has graduated. The walls are glass. The desks are real. The company has a name now, visible in frosted lettering on the glass: something bland, something corporate. "Synthesis Dynamics" or "Convergence Capital" — the kind of name that tells you nothing.

Kirk stands at the head of a conference table. Six people seated. They don't look Borg — they look like middle management. Polo shirts and khakis. But they sit identically. They listen identically. They take notes at the same moment.

On the screen behind Kirk: a document. Not a map. Not a directory. A *style guide*.

**KIRK:** *(reading from the screen)* "When contacting a recruitment target, use the phrase 'leadership opportunity' — not 'career change,' not 'new role,' not 'position.' The phrase is 'leadership opportunity.' When describing the organization, use 'aligned network' — not 'company,' not 'group,' not 'team.' Every communication follows this lexicon. Every interaction uses the same words."

He pauses. Turns to the room.

**KIRK:** Why?

**AIDE:** *(robotically eager)* Consistency creates trust. The target hears the same language from multiple contacts and concludes it's an institution, not a scheme.

**KIRK:** That's one reason. The deeper reason: when everyone uses the same words, you can search for those words. Our monitoring tools flag "leadership opportunity" across every platform — email, text, social media. If someone uses the phrase, they're ours. If they use a different phrase, they're freelancing.

He writes on the whiteboard: **LEXICON**.

**KIRK:** One language. One vocabulary. One set of canonical phrases. Everything searchable, everything standardized, everyone interchangeable.

He adds a number below the word: **6 / 31**.

A new city on the map — somewhere east. Chicago? Boston? The Midwest is opening up.

**KIRK:** *(turning back to the screen)* Page two. Response protocols. When a target says "I'll think about it..."

The camera pulls back through the glass wall. The conference room shrinks. The building is one of hundreds in a business park. Identical buildings. Identical glass. Identical frosted names. From the air, they look like slots in a table.

SMASH TO BLACK.

### Why This Works

The stinger rhymes with the A-plot at every level:

- **Interning = Canonical copies.** The team interns strings — one canonical copy of "Faber," shared by all instances. Kirk interns *language* — one canonical phrase of "leadership opportunity," shared by all operatives. Same operation, opposite purpose. The team's interning preserves individual meaning while sharing the word. Kirk's standardization erases individual meaning by imposing the word.

- **Searchability through standardization.** Kirk explicitly states the reason for canonical language: it makes his network searchable. If everyone uses the same phrase, monitoring tools can find them instantly. This is interning-as-surveillance: the canonical form enables O(1) detection. The team's string interning enables O(1) comparison — both are "compare by pointer instead of compare by content." Kirk just uses it to find his people.

- **The "LEXICON."** A direct parallel to "TABULARIUM" from 1x05. Kirk labels his tools. He names his operations. The audience is accumulating Kirk's terminology: TABULARIUM, LEXICON. He's building a vocabulary for his shadow system.

- **The conference room.** Kirk has upgraded from strip mall to co-working space to real office. The setting tracks his growth. Six seated people, all identical in posture — a visual echo of the Borg. The audience sees assimilation in business-casual.

- **Nominalist interning vs. realist interning.** The team interns strings: the word "Faber" is canonical because it IS the word "Faber" — the canonical form respects what the word already is. Kirk interns phrases: "leadership opportunity" is canonical because Kirk DECIDED it is — the canonical form is imposed from above, arbitrary, replaceable with any other phrase. Same mechanism. Opposite philosophy. Realist canonical: the form follows nature. Nominalist canonical: the form follows authority.

## 3. C-PLOT THREADING: Four Beats

### Beat C1 (Cold Open or Early Act 1): The Acquisition

O'Neill and Duncan reviewing intelligence. Brief, efficient, establishing.

**O'NEILL:** Three more acquisitions this week. A logistics startup in Portland, a data analytics firm in Denver, and a consulting group in — *(checks)* — Boise.

**DUNCAN:** Boise?

**O'NEILL:** Kirk's not building a tech company. He's building an organization. Logistics, analytics, consulting — these are capabilities, not businesses.

**DUNCAN:** He's not buying products. He's buying *functions*.

The word lands. Functions. Not people, not companies — functions. The same nominalist categorization Kirk used in 1x05's directory ("Infrastructure," "Signal Processing," "Recruitment"). Now he's *acquiring* by category. The hash table indexed his existing network; now he's shopping to fill gaps in the table.

**DUNCAN:** *(quiet, the Immortal who watches patterns across centuries)* I've seen this before.

**O'NEILL:** When?

**DUNCAN:** Every empire that tried to grow by absorbing.

He doesn't say "the Borg." He doesn't know about the Borg yet (not in detail). But the pattern is old enough that a 400-year-old swordsman recognizes it.

### Beat C2 (During Act 2 or 3): The Compass Update

Carter checking her data. The compass. A new data point.

Midas walks past. The needle spins — but differently this time. Instead of a full 360-degree rotation, it stutters. Half-rotation. Pause. Quarter-rotation. Pause. Returns to north.

Carter marks it. Compares to her notes from 1x05. The spin pattern is changing. The field around Midas isn't constant — it *fluctuates*.

**CARTER:** *(to herself, writing)* Variable amplitude. The effect isn't decaying — it's oscillating.

She looks across the courtyard at Midas, who is laughing about something with Data. Completely unaware. The most cheerful temporal anomaly in the galaxy.

Carter puts the compass down. Picks up a second instrument — she's brought more equipment. A small device that measures electromagnetic field strength. She places it in a spot she's marked with chalk. When Midas passes, she'll have two data sources instead of one.

**DIRECTION NOTE:** The audience is watching Carter build an experiment. She's doing what Pliny does — observe, record, don't theorize yet. But the data set is growing. By 1x08, she'll have weeks of readings. By 1x13, she'll have a theory. The slow build is the point.

### Beat C3 (Act 3 or 4): Duncan Recognizes Kirk's Pattern

Duncan has been quiet since the morning intelligence review. He's been thinking about the acquisitions. He finds O'Neill after the main technical work.

**DUNCAN:** The Portland startup Kirk acquired — what was their specialty?

**O'NEILL:** Internal communications platforms. Workplace messaging.

**DUNCAN:** And the Denver firm?

**O'NEILL:** Data normalization. Taking messy data from multiple sources and making it consistent.

**DUNCAN:** *(slowly)* Communications. Normalization. He bought the ability to make everyone talk the same way, and the ability to make all the data look the same.

**O'NEILL:** So?

**DUNCAN:** He's not indexing anymore. He's *standardizing*. He's past the point where he needs to find his people. Now he needs them all to speak the same language.

The mirror of the A-plot. The team is interning strings — making words canonical so that comparison is instant. Kirk is standardizing communication — making language canonical so that his network operates as one.

**O'NEILL:** *(grim)* Same thing the team built today. One copy of each word.

**DUNCAN:** Except the team chose which words already existed. Kirk is choosing which words *will* exist.

This is the core distinction: the team's interning respects existing reality (the string "Faber" already IS "Faber"). Kirk's standardizing imposes a new reality ("From now on, say 'leadership opportunity'"). Realist interning vs. nominalist standardization. Duncan sees it because Duncan has watched empires standardize cultures for four centuries.

### Beat C4 (Post-climax): Midas and Data — The Anomaly Deepens

A quiet scene. Late. Midas and Data sitting together — their recurring friendship scene. Data is reading. Midas is fiddling with something.

**MIDAS:** Data. Your eyes.

**DATA:** What about them?

**MIDAS:** They were green when you arrived. Now they're... more green some days than others. Have you noticed?

**DATA:** I have not. Green is my standard ocular pigmentation, though I acknowledge the lighting on Nova Roma differs from—

**MIDAS:** *(casually)* Yesterday they were almost gold.

Data pauses. This is the first indication — subtle, easily dismissed — that proximity to Midas is affecting Data too. Not temporally (Data has no biology to disrupt) but *chromatically*. Midas's temporal field is interacting with Data's optical systems.

This is a new data point for the audience (not yet for Carter, who doesn't know). It plants a seed for 1x13 and 2x11-13: Midas's effect isn't limited to magnetic compasses. It affects technology. It affects *Data*. The cheerful anomaly is more anomalous than anyone realizes.

**DATA:** I will run a self-diagnostic.

**MIDAS:** *(already moved on)* Do you want more wine?

The scene ends warm. Two friends. One made of circuits, one leaking time. Neither knows what they are to each other yet.

## 4. BLACK STONE / CYBELE: Methos's Alexandria Revelation

### The Setup

The season outline is explicit: in 1x06, Methos accidentally reveals knowledge of Alexandria, and Daniel pushes. This is the first major Black Stone beat in the present-day timeline — moving from 1x05's three-second seed to a proper scene.

### Placement

Mid-to-late episode. During or after the Borg/interning discussion — when Methos is comparing assimilation to interning. The conversation naturally drifts toward historical precedent, which is Methos's territory. Daniel, the archaeologist, asks the question that cracks open the door.

### The Scene

The team is discussing interning's philosophical implications. Methos, characteristically, provides the historical frame:

**METHOS:** Every empire has tried this. One language, one system, one canonical form. Alexander imposed Greek. Rome imposed Latin. The British imposed English. The efficiency argument is always the same: "We need a common tongue."

**DANIEL:** And each time, something was lost.

**METHOS:** *(carefully)* Not always. Sometimes the common tongue preserved what would have been lost. The Rosetta Stone exists because Greek was the canonical language of Ptolemaic Egypt. Without it, we'd have lost the hieroglyphs entirely.

**DANIEL:** You say "we" like you weren't—

He stops. Daniel has been watching Methos for months. The way Methos talks about ancient history isn't academic. It's *eyewitness*. Daniel hasn't pushed this before. Now, in the context of a conversation about preservation and loss, he pushes.

**DANIEL:** You were in Alexandria.

It's not a question. Methos goes still. The wine glass doesn't move. Five thousand years of practiced deflection calibrate the response.

**METHOS:** Lots of people were in Alexandria.

**DANIEL:** Not when you were there.

Long beat. Methos evaluates. Daniel is a linguist, an archaeologist, a scholar who has spent his career reconstructing lost knowledge from fragments. He's the person on Nova Roma most capable of understanding — and most dangerous to confide in, because Daniel will *pursue*.

**METHOS:** *(choosing each word)* I was in Alexandria. More than once. The last time was... difficult.

**DANIEL:** The burning?

**METHOS:** Not the burning everyone thinks of. A different loss. Slower. Less dramatic. *(setting down his wine)* There was a library within the library. The scholars who ran it kept things in the public collection — scrolls, maps, mathematical texts. Extraordinary work. And then there was a sub-chamber. Below the main floor. Stone walls, cold, dry. The kind of room built to preserve things for longer than any library lasts.

**DANIEL:** What was in it?

**METHOS:** Artifacts. Things that didn't fit the categories the scholars used. Things that predated the library by... a long time. And there was a priestess who guarded the chamber. She was not a scholar. She was something older.

His expression has shifted — the sardonic mask dropping for a moment. This is the man who watched the Cybele cult in Alexandria, who saw the ecstatic rites, who knows what the Stone does to people. He's not telling Daniel everything. But he's telling him more than he's told anyone in centuries.

**METHOS:** There were cults in Alexandria that the scholars didn't document. Cults that were older than the library. Older than Egypt.

**DANIEL:** *(leaning forward — this is his life's work, lost knowledge, undocumented cults)* What kind of cults?

**METHOS:** *(pulling back — the mask returning)* The kind that makes people... different. *(beat)* I've said more than I should.

He picks up his wine. Changes the subject. Returns to the technical discussion — "Your interning system is elegant, by the way. One copy. Canonical. The Ptolemies would have loved it."

Daniel notices the deflection. Files it. He won't push further today. But he has a new thread to pull: a sub-chamber, a priestess, something older than Egypt, cults that made people different. Daniel is an archaeologist. He pulls threads.

### What the Audience Gets

- **First confirmation** that Methos's 1x05 reaction (the compass, the recognition) is connected to something specific: Alexandria, a sub-chamber, artifacts, a priestess, cults that change people.
- **First mention** of the sub-chamber and its guardians. The word "priestess" connects to "Cybele" for mythology-literate viewers. The "older than Egypt" tag connects to Phrygia for geography-literate viewers.
- **The deflection** is as important as the revelation. Methos says "I've said more than I should" — which means there's MORE. The audience knows they got the surface. The deep water is coming.
- **Daniel as investigator.** Daniel is now actively tracking a thread that won't pay off until 1x13 (Midas flashback reveals the Phrygian stone) and 1x17 (Methos's own flashback — the full sub-chamber, the stone that whispers). Daniel the archaeologist pursuing a five-thousand-year-old mystery through living testimony.

### Thread Connection: Interning as Context

The revelation happens during the interning discussion because *interning is the philosophical bridge*. The team is discussing canonical copies — one true version of each string. Methos's revelation is about canonical objects — one true artifact in a sub-chamber, guarded across millennia. The priestess *interned* the stone: one canonical copy, one location, one guardian. The parallel is between computational interning and ritual preservation.

This also foreshadows the Cybele cult's approach: they keep ONE stone. They move it, guard it, but it's always ONE artifact. The cult has been interning the Black Stone across history — canonical form, different locations, same essence. The deepest parallel in the episode: the team interns strings, the cult interns the stone. Same operation. Very different purposes.

## 6. NOMINALISM / REALISM: Interning as the Deepest Realism

### The Core Analysis

String interning is the most explicitly realist operation the team has built so far. Here's why:

**The realist claim:** Things have natures. Two instances of the word "Faber" are not merely similar — they ARE the same word. They share a nature. Interning *recognizes* this: instead of storing two independent copies that happen to have the same characters, it stores ONE canonical copy and points both references to it. The canonical copy IS the nature of the word made concrete.

This is the moderate realist position articulated as a data structure: universals are real and can be instantiated. The word "Faber" exists as a universal (the canonical copy). Individual uses of "Faber" are instances that participate in the universal (pointers to the canonical copy). The universal isn't separate from the instances (it's in the same memory), but it's logically prior to them (it was stored first, they point to it).

### Dramatic Expression

**CARTER:** *(explaining interning to the team)* We have three hundred census entries for citizens whose trade is "Faber." Three hundred separate copies of the same five letters. That's fifteen hundred bytes of memory holding the same word three hundred times.

**PLINY:** *(the polymath who wrote "Faber" in his encyclopedia decades ago)* They are all the same word. They should be one word.

**CARTER:** That's exactly what interning does. One canonical copy. Every reference points to it. When we compare, we don't compare the letters — we compare the *locations*. If two references point to the same place, they are the same word. Instantly. No reading required.

**LUCIA:** *(quiet, precise)* You are saying that identity is determined by nature, not by inspection. Two instances of "Faber" do not need to be compared letter by letter — they ARE the same word, and the system recognizes this structurally.

**CARTER:** *(surprised by the philosophical precision)* That's... a very good way to put it.

**LUCIA:** *(archly)* In my language, we have always known this. A rose is a rose regardless of which garden it grows in. You are now teaching your machine the same thing.

### The Borg Mirror

The Borg also reduce many to one. But the mechanism and philosophy are opposite:

**Interning (Nova Roma):**
- Many copies → one canonical form
- The canonical form already existed (the string "Faber" was "Faber" before interning)
- The sources remain distinct (Citizen A and Citizen B both point to "Faber" but are still different citizens)
- The operation *recognizes* shared nature
- The efficiency comes from *truth*: they really are the same word

**Assimilation (Borg):**
- Many individuals → one Collective
- The canonical form is imposed (you will be designated "Seven of Nine, Tertiary Adjunct")
- The sources are destroyed (the individual's identity is overwritten)
- The operation *imposes* shared nature
- The efficiency comes from *force*: they will be made the same

**METHOS:** *(the 5,000-year-old who has watched empires assimilate cultures)* The Borg make everything identical. You make things *canonical*. The difference is that your method requires the things to already share a nature. The Borg's method requires them to surrender their nature.

**DATA:** *(precisely)* The distinction is between recognition and imposition. The interning system recognizes that two instances of "Faber" are already the same word. The Borg impose that two individuals are the same drone. In one case, the canonical form follows from nature. In the other, the canonical form replaces nature.

**CASEY:** *(from his workbench, not looking up)* One for all isn't the same as all for one.

The episode title — "Unum Pro Omnibus" — resonates differently after this exchange. "One for all" = one canonical copy serving all references (interning). "All for one" = all individuals serving one Collective (assimilation). The title contains both readings. The episode explores why they're opposites.

### Kirk's Stinger as Nominalist Interning

Kirk's standardization (from the stinger) completes the triangle:

| | Team (Interning) | Kirk (Standardizing) | Borg (Assimilating) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **What's unified** | A word | A phrase | A person |
| **The canonical form** | Already existed | Imposed by authority | Imposed by force |
| **The sources** | Remain distinct | Become interchangeable | Are destroyed |
| **Purpose** | Efficient comparison | Efficient monitoring | Efficient expansion |
| **Philosophy** | Realist: nature determines form | Nominalist: authority determines form | Anti-realist: form replaces nature |

Kirk is the middle position — not yet Borg, but moving toward them. He imposes canonical phrases (nominalist), but his operatives are still individual humans (not yet drones). The audience watches the gradient from interning to assimilation and understands: Kirk's standardization is the intermediate stage. First you standardize the language. Then you standardize the people. Then there's nothing left to standardize because there's nothing left that's different.

## 7. BACKWARD AND FORWARD CONNECTIONS

### Backward: What This Episode Draws On

**1x01 (Piscina):** The interned strings are stored in the Piscina. The arena allocator now serves three layers: the Xar (1x04) holds collections, the Tabula Dispersa (1x05) indexes them, and the Internamentum (1x06) canonicalizes their keys. The dependency chain:

```
Piscina (memory, 1x01)
  -> Chorda (text, 1x03)
  -> Xar (collection, 1x04)
  -> Tabula Dispersa (index, 1x05)
  -> Internamentum (canonical copies, 1x06)
```

Each layer stands on the one beneath it. The audience should feel the accretion — the foundation from episode one supporting increasingly sophisticated structures.

**1x03 (Chorda):** The strings being interned ARE Chordae — length-prefixed, not null-terminated. The interning system uses the Chorda's known length to compute hashes efficiently, and the Chorda's data to allocate exact-size canonical copies. This is 1x03's realist design (a string that knows its own length) enabling 1x06's realist operation (a string system that recognizes shared nature). The decisions stack.

**1x05 (Tabula Dispersa):** The interning system IS a hash table — the Tabula Dispersa from 1x05, repurposed. The key is the string, the value is the pointer to the canonical copy. The hash function (friatio) from 1x05 computes the bucket. The audience sees the hash table not as an isolated tool but as *infrastructure* — a building block reused for a new purpose. This is the library dependency chain working as intended.

**1x05 (Pliny's "I have more questions"):** The faith thread rests. But Pliny's relationship with Casey — the scholarly curiosity, the "more evenings" — is background warmth. They're working together. The questions will come later. The friendship deepens through shared work, not through theological dialogue.

**1x05 (Methos's 3-second reaction):** The seed planted in the background of 1x05's celebration now grows into a scene. Methos's recognition — the compass, the feeling of something ancient — pays off in his Alexandria revelation. The audience who caught the 1x05 seed gets the satisfaction of seeing it sprout.

### Forward: What This Episode Plants

**For 1x07 (Filum / File I/O):** Interned strings exist only in memory. They're canonical, efficient, fast — but volatile. Power failure loses them all. The need to *persist* the interning table (and everything else) motivates file I/O. Methos's fire warning from 1x05's tag still echoes: "When the fire comes — and the fire always comes — what survives?"

**For 1x08 (Horologium / Tempus):** Carter's growing temporal data set (compass + clock drift) needs reliable timestamps. But the clocks disagree. The time library is motivated by the need to make Carter's anomaly measurements trustworthy.

**For 1x09 (Arbor / XML):** Interned strings are flat — just words. But Pliny's encyclopedia has *structure*: minerals belong to geology, geology belongs to the natural sciences, the natural sciences belong to knowledge. The flat interned keys need hierarchical context. The XML tree provides it.

**For 1x13 (Aurum / Midas flashback):** Daniel now has a thread to pull: Alexandria, sub-chamber, artifacts, a priestess, cults that change people. The audience has: Methos's 1x05 reaction + Methos's 1x06 revelation + compass data + clock drift + Data's eyes changing. Eight episodes of accumulated clues converge in the Midas flashback, which answers the question the audience has been indexing toward since 1x05.

**For 1x17 (Quinque Milia Annorum / Methos flashback):** Methos's "I've said more than I should" explicitly creates an expectation of more revelation. When 1x17 delivers the full flashback — the sub-chamber, the stone, the priests who heard whispers — the audience will remember that Daniel was given the first fragment here. Eleven episodes of Daniel pulling the thread.

**For S5 (Interning vs. Assimilating):** The thematic distinction established in 1x06 — canonical vs. identical, recognition vs. imposition — runs through the entire series. When the Borg fleet arrives in 5x11-12 and the assimilation attempt fails because the infrastructure is built on entity sovereignty and canonical integrity, the root of that victory is HERE. The interning philosophy — things that share a nature can share a canonical form without losing their individuality — is the philosophical immune system at its most basic level.

## APPENDIX: Title Resonance — "Unum Pro Omnibus"

### Three Readings

**1. Technical (Interning):** One canonical copy for all references. "Unum Pro Omnibus" = one copy serves all. The efficiency of shared truth.

**2. Theological (The Cross):** "One for all" = "A man who died for everyone" (Casey's line from 1x05). The crucifix in frame during the canonical discussion. The audience makes the connection: the most canonical act in Casey's theology is one death that serves all of humanity. One sacrifice. Universal application. The ultimate interning — one event, canonical, pointed to by every believer.

**3. Borg (Assimilation):** "One for all" = the Collective. One will imposed on all. The Borg's version: "One mind for all drones." The same phrase, inverted. The title contains both the thesis and the antithesis.

The audience doesn't need to hold all three readings simultaneously. But attentive viewers will notice that "Unum Pro Omnibus" describes both the interning system and the crucifix and the Borg — and that the difference between them is the entire philosophical argument of the series.

### The "Tres Moschettarii" Easter Egg

"Unum Pro Omnibus" is half of the Three Musketeers motto: "Unus pro omnibus, omnes pro uno" — One for all, all for one. The show uses only the first half. The second half — "all for one" — is the Borg's version. Leaving it unspoken is the point. The team builds the first half. The Borg demand the second.

Genre-literate fans will catch this. Dumas fans will love it. The show is built for the people who notice.
